## 1. Macro Summary

The week's dominant story is one that would have seemed implausible eighteen months ago: oil is back above $100 a barrel, after President Trump announced a US blockade of the Strait of Hormuz following the collapse of Iran peace talks. Roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil passes through that narrow channel. When it gets threatened — let alone physically blocked — energy markets don't wait for confirmation. They panic first and ask questions later. The price spike is immediate; the geopolitical resolution, if it comes, is always slower.

For you personally, this matters in a few overlapping ways. Higher oil feeds directly into transport, manufacturing, and food costs — inflation that central banks find particularly awkward because they can't drill their way out of it with interest rates. The Swiss franc, your day-to-day currency, tends to strengthen in genuine risk-off episodes, which cushions some of the blow on imported energy costs. Sterling, however, is more exposed. Britain imports a meaningful share of its energy and has limited fiscal room to absorb another supply shock. Meanwhile, the US bond market is already flashing warnings: 10-year Treasury yields at 4.31% and 30-year at 4.91% may look contained on paper, but Wolf Street's read — that yields are *too low* given the inflation trajectory — is not a fringe view. The Fed has quietly made peace with core PCE above 3%. That is not a neutral fact. It is a policy choice with consequences for every asset class you hold.

One additional footnote worth watching: the UK government is apparently planning to align with EU single market rules via new legislation that could bypass full parliamentary scrutiny. The constitutional question is real, but the economic one is equally interesting — quiet regulatory convergence with Europe, pursued through the back door, could meaningfully shift the investment case for British domestics over the medium term. More on that as detail emerges.

## 2. Stock Watch

**[XOM] — ExxonMobil**
- **Why:** A sustained oil price above $100 dramatically expands free cash flow, and Exxon's integrated model means it benefits across the upstream/downstream chain.
- **Bear Case:** A rapid diplomatic resolution to the Hormuz standoff — or Saudi overproduction to calm markets — could send oil back below $80 within weeks, gutting the near-term earnings upgrade cycle.

**[TAN] — Invesco Solar ETF**
- **Why:** Energy security anxiety accelerates the political and commercial case for renewables across Europe and Asia, historically a reliable tailwind for solar infrastructure spending.
- **Bear Case:** Rising Treasury yields increase the discount rate on long-duration clean energy projects; if the 30-year pushes toward 5.5%, many solar developments simply stop pencilling out.

**[BP] — BP plc**
- **Why:** As a UK-listed major with significant Hormuz-region exposure, BP captures both the oil price windfall and any eventual UK-EU reset that stabilises the British investment environment.
- **Bear Case:** BP's ongoing strategic pivot away from hydrocarbons leaves it structurally less leveraged to an oil spike than pure-play peers — the worst of both worlds if the transition stalls.
