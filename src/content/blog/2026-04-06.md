## 1. Macro Summary

The dominant story this week sits at the intersection of geopolitics and energy markets, and it is not subtle. Trump has issued Iran an ultimatum — reopen the Strait of Hormuz by Tuesday or face military strikes on power plants and bridges. The deadline has already shifted once, which tells you something about the reliability of the theatre, but oil markets are not waiting for certainty. UK small businesses reliant on heating oil are already rationing fuel, and the Federation of Small Businesses reports energy bills set to more than double. For context: roughly 20% of global oil supply and a third of liquefied natural gas transits the Strait. A prolonged closure would not be a price shock — it would be a supply shock, which is considerably worse and considerably harder for central banks to address.

Layered on top of this is a bond market that was already getting uncomfortable before anyone mentioned Hormuz. The US 10-year Treasury yield bounced back to 4.35% last week, the 30-year is approaching 5%, and the entire yield curve now sits above the Fed Funds rate — a configuration that historically signals the market is pricing in either more inflation, more debt issuance, or both. The blunt translation: the US government's borrowing costs are rising, which makes the already-ballooning debt pile more expensive to service, which increases the pressure to issue even more bonds, which pushes yields higher still. It is not a doom loop yet. But it rhymes with one. Meanwhile, the US labour market is doing something genuinely strange: job creation is weak and federal employment is being cut, yet the unemployment rate is not rising because workers are simply leaving the labour force. Fewer people competing for fewer jobs is not the same as a healthy economy — it is just a different kind of stagnation.

For a British expat holding CHF, GBP, and any USD-denominated assets, the near-term read is straightforward if uncomfortable: energy-driven inflation is not done, real yields in the US are rising (bad for growth stocks, mixed for the dollar), and the Swiss franc — that reliable refuge — will likely continue attracting safe-haven flows if the Hormuz situation deteriorates further. The SNB will be watching with its customary Alpine inscrutability.

## 2. Stock Watch

**[MSFT] — Microsoft**
- **Why:** Rotations into value have pressured the stock, but Microsoft's enterprise AI integration across Azure and Office 365 gives it a recurring-revenue moat that holds up better in stagflationary environments than pure-growth peers.
- **Bear Case:** Rising long-duration yields compress the multiples of every mega-cap tech name; if the 10-year pushes toward 4.75%, MSFT's premium valuation becomes much harder to justify.

**[NVDA] — Nvidia**
- **Why:** Data centre capex from hyperscalers shows little sign of cooling, and Nvidia retains pricing power on GPU clusters that no competitor has meaningfully threatened.
- **Bear Case:** Any escalation in US–Iran conflict risks disrupting semiconductor supply chains through energy cost spikes and shipping disruption; Nvidia is also the most crowded long trade on the planet.

**[AAPL] — Apple**
- **Why:** Q1 2026's rotation into dividend and value stocks arguably benefits Apple more than its peers given its buyback programme and relatively defensive consumer base.
- **Bear Case:** Apple's manufacturing concentration in Asia combined with ongoing tariff uncertainty means a geopolitical flare-up could land directly on its cost structure with little warning.
