## 1. Macro Summary

If you want to understand the global economy right now, picture a patient recovering from one illness (tariff-induced trade shock) while simultaneously catching another (an oil crisis courtesy of the Iran war). The S&P 500 limped out of Q1 down 4.6%, its worst quarterly start since 2025, before staging a modest rally on 1 April as President Trump hinted US forces could leave Iran within "two or three weeks." Markets, ever the optimists, took this as gospel. Whether the Strait of Hormuz agrees is another matter entirely.

The real headline is oil. Brent crude is hovering near $110–120 a barrel — levels not seen since 2008 — after the closure of the Strait of Hormuz choked roughly a fifth of the world's petroleum supply. The IEA has warned that April will be "much worse than March," with supply losses expected to double by mid-month. Goldman Sachs forecasts Brent averaging $110 through April. For those of us who remember the 1970s energy crisis only from textbooks, we are now getting a practical refresher course. The knock-on effects are straightforward: higher petrol prices, higher shipping costs, and inflation that central banks thought they had tamed creeping back through the kitchen door.

Meanwhile, the US effective tariff rate has reached its highest level since 1938, adding an estimated 1.3% to the price level and costing the average American household roughly $2,100. The combination of supply-side oil shocks and demand-side tariff costs has pushed recession odds to their highest in years. As Hitchens might have observed, the remarkable thing about shooting yourself in both feet simultaneously is the symmetry.

## 2. Stock Watch

**XOM — ExxonMobil**
- **Why:** The obvious beneficiary of $110+ Brent. US onshore production is unaffected by the Hormuz closure, and Exxon's integrated model (upstream + refining) captures margin at multiple points in the supply chain.
- **Bear Case:** If Trump's "two or three weeks" prediction proves accurate and the Strait reopens, oil could drop 30% in days. Exxon's valuation already prices in elevated crude — a peace dividend would be a share-price headwind.

**LMT — Lockheed Martin**
- **Why:** The world's largest defence contractor is the prime beneficiary of the Iran conflict and rising NATO spending commitments across Europe. The F-35 programme alone provides decades of revenue visibility.
- **Bear Case:** Defence stocks are priced for perpetual conflict. Any credible ceasefire or diplomatic breakthrough would trigger a sharp rotation out of the sector. Political risk also looms: budget sequestration or a change of administration could slow procurement.

**FANG — Diamondback Energy**
- **Why:** A pure-play Permian Basin oil producer capturing the full benefit of elevated crude prices without Middle Eastern operational risk. Analysts expect elevated earnings throughout 2026.
- **Bear Case:** Diamondback's hedging book may cap upside if prices stay elevated, and any rapid oil price decline would hit pure-play producers hardest. Permian Basin production growth is also attracting regulatory scrutiny over water usage and flaring.

**HWM — Howmet Aerospace**
- **Why:** Supplies lightweight metal components to both commercial aviation and military programmes. Consensus estimates imply 19.7% earnings growth in 2026, driven by defence demand and an aviation aftermarket recovery.
- **Bear Case:** A recession triggered by the oil shock would hammer commercial aviation orders. Howmet's military revenue alone may not sustain the current multiple if civilian demand falls off a cliff.

## 3. The Expat Angle

Sterling is trading at roughly 1.045 against the franc today — essentially flat for the past fortnight and offering British expats in Switzerland precious little relief. The Bank of England held rates in March and markets no longer expect a cut in 2026, as UK CPI remains sticky at 3.0% with energy costs threatening to push it higher. Across the border, the SNB is parked at 0% with Swiss inflation at a mere 0.5%, reflecting the franc's enduring safe-haven status. The divergence matters for your wallet: with UK inflation running six times higher than Switzerland's, the real purchasing power of any sterling income you hold is eroding meaningfully faster than your Swiss-franc expenses. If you are transferring pounds to pay Zurich rent, the maths is working against you. The one sliver of good news is that the SNB is unlikely to hike before 2027, so mortgage holders on variable Swiss rates can breathe easy — for now.

## 4. Critical Risks

- **Strait of Hormuz escalation:** The IEA warns April supply losses will double. Any further disruption could push Brent past $130 and trigger a global recession.
- **Ceasefire whiplash:** Markets rallied on Trump's withdrawal comments. If diplomacy stalls, the sell-off could be sharper than the rally.
- **UK stagflation:** With inflation at 3%, rates on hold, and GDP growth forecast at under 1%, the UK risks a prolonged period of stagnation — bad news for sterling earners.
- **US tariff retaliation:** The EU and Canada are deepening trade ties that exclude the US. A further escalation in retaliatory tariffs could accelerate deglobalisation and hurt multinationals.
- **Swiss franc strength:** If risk aversion spikes, the franc could appreciate sharply, making Zurich even more expensive for expats while squeezing Swiss exporters.
