## 1. Macro Summary

The dominant story this week — and, frankly, this quarter — is the economic fallout from the Iran conflict. Oil prices have climbed to levels not seen in years, with Brent crude hovering near $112 per barrel and WTI at $115.48 as of yesterday. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has disrupted roughly 20% of global oil supply, which the International Energy Agency has called the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." That is not hyperbole designed to sell newspapers; it is a straightforward description of a chokepoint through which a fifth of the world's oil used to flow, now effectively shut. Energy-dependent European economies — Germany and Italy in particular — are staring down the barrel of stagflation, and the ECB has said as much publicly. If you were hoping for a quiet spring, the Middle East had other plans.

Meanwhile, the US trade war grinds on in the background like a headache you've learned to live with. After the Supreme Court struck down the administration's use of emergency powers for sweeping tariffs in February, a 15% universal tariff was reimposed under a different legal authority. The effective US tariff rate now sits at 11%, the highest since 1943. American households are paying over $1,000 more annually for everyday goods, and companies like Apple have absorbed nearly a billion dollars per quarter in tariff costs. Global GDP growth is forecast at 2.7–2.9% for 2026 — respectable on paper, but masking a world where inflation has reversed its cooling trend and consumer confidence is fragile.

## 2. Stock Watch

**ARMK — Aramark**
- **Why:** JPMorgan recently added Aramark back to its top picks after the company posted a "well-anchored" 2026 outlook. As a food, uniform, and facilities provider, it benefits from steady institutional demand — hospitals, universities, and offices still need to feed people regardless of geopolitics.
- **Bear Case:** Aramark's margins are directly exposed to food and energy inflation. With oil above $110, transport and ingredient costs could squeeze profitability hard, and institutional clients may renegotiate contracts downward.

**IWM — iShares Russell 2000 ETF**
- **Why:** Small-cap domestic US stocks have outperformed the Nasdaq by nearly 9% over the past month. The logic is simple: smaller companies with less foreign exposure benefit from reshoring trends and are less vulnerable to tariff headwinds hitting multinationals.
- **Bear Case:** Small caps are historically more sensitive to recessions. If the oil shock tips the US economy into a downturn, these companies — often more leveraged and less diversified — will get hit first and hardest.

**VXUS — Vanguard Total International Stock ETF**
- **Why:** International equities have returned 27% over the past year, actually outpacing the S&P 500. Diversification away from US-centric portfolios has finally paid off, driven partly by dollar weakness and strong performance in emerging markets.
- **Bear Case:** A prolonged Hormuz closure would hammer European and Asian economies that are far more dependent on Middle Eastern energy than the US. The 27% gain could unwind quickly if the conflict escalates.

**XLE — Energy Select Sector SPDR**
- **Why:** With oil above $110, US shale producers are minting money. JPMorgan has rotated out of natural gas names in favour of shale oil producers, viewing them as "relative winners" in the current conflict environment.
- **Bear Case:** Energy stocks are priced for sustained high oil. Any ceasefire, diplomatic breakthrough, or reopening of the Strait would send crude tumbling — and energy equities with it. You're essentially betting the war continues, which is an uncomfortable wager.

## 3. The Expat Angle

Here is the genuinely useful bit if you're earning francs in Zurich: the Swiss franc is, once again, doing what it does best — being boring and strong. Safe-haven demand has pushed the CHF higher as investors flee conflict-driven volatility, and GBP/CHF is trading around 1.045, meaning your francs stretch further than usual if you're sending money back to the UK. The SNB held its policy rate at 0% in March and is expected to stay there through the year, with Swiss inflation forecast at just 0.5% — a number that would make most central bankers weep with envy. The catch, as always, is that official inflation figures don't capture what actually hurts your wallet in Zurich. Krankenkasse premiums rose 4.4% this year to an average of CHF 393 per month, following increases of 8.7% and 6% in the two prior years. That is a cumulative squeeze of roughly 20% in three years on a bill you cannot avoid. Meanwhile, the oil shock has yet to fully feed through to Swiss consumer prices — energy costs tend to arrive with a delay, so expect grocery and transport bills to creep upward through Q2. The franc's strength is your shield, but it is not impenetrable.

## 4. Critical Risks

- **Strait of Hormuz escalation:** Any widening of the Iran conflict could push oil well beyond $120, triggering genuine recession in energy-importing economies across Europe and Asia.

- **US tariff ratchet:** The 15% universal tariff could be expanded or supplemented. With the legal basis now shifted to the Trade Act rather than emergency powers, Congress has fewer tools to block further escalation.

- **European stagflation:** The ECB has flagged the risk of simultaneous stagnation and inflation. If Germany enters technical recession, the knock-on effects for Switzerland's export sector — and by extension, the labour market — would be significant.

- **SNB policy trap:** With rates already at 0% and a stated aversion to going negative again, the SNB has limited ammunition if the franc appreciates too aggressively or the economy slows. They're essentially hoping they won't need to act, which is not a strategy so much as a prayer.

- **Krankenkasse compounding:** Three consecutive years of above-inflation premium increases are eroding disposable income for Swiss residents. If 2027 brings another 4%+ hike, the political pressure for structural reform will become impossible to ignore — but reform itself carries transition risks.
