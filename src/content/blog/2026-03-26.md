## 1. Macro Summary

The big story of the week isn't a company, a central bank, or even an election — it's a courtroom decision that's quietly reshaping the entire logic of global trade. In February, the US Supreme Court ruled that the President cannot use a Cold War-era emergency powers law (known as IEEPA) to unilaterally impose sweeping tariffs. Rather than accept the ruling as a check on executive power — as one might naively hope — the administration promptly pivoted to a different statute: Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, which allows tariffs of up to 15% on all imported goods for up to 150 days. The effective average tariff rate now sits at around 10.5%, the highest since 1943. For China specifically, it reaches 33.9%. This is not a trade spat. This is the largest US tax increase as a share of GDP since 1993, and the cost — an estimated $1,500 per American household annually — is beginning to filter through to prices on shop shelves.

Meanwhile, markets are oscillating between hope and anxiety over US-Iran ceasefire talks, which sent oil prices lower earlier this week and briefly lifted equities. The Dow gained 305 points on Wednesday, and the S&P 500 closed at 6,591. That's encouraging — but the relationship between "stocks up" and "things are fine" has rarely been shakier. As Goldman Sachs notes, global growth is still expected at a respectable 2.8% in 2026, but forecasters have simultaneously raised inflation projections and lowered growth projections in the same breath, which is the economic equivalent of being told your house is structurally sound but also slightly on fire.

The tariff regime also threatens to accelerate the fragmentation of global supply chains that has been quietly underway since 2018. New Section 301 investigations have been opened against China, Vietnam, Taiwan, Mexico, Japan, the EU, and dozens of others. If you've ever wondered what a world that trades less with itself looks like, you're about to find out.

## 2. Stock Watch

**ARM — Arm Holdings**
- **Why:** Raymond James upgraded Arm to "outperform" this week after the company unveiled its first in-house central processing unit chip — a significant step from licensing chip designs to actually building the silicon. In an AI data centre boom, that's a potentially lucrative pivot.
- **Bear Case:** ARM is already priced for near-perfection after years of AI-fuelled enthusiasm. If data centre spending growth moderates, or if NVIDIA or AMD muscle in on the same CPU territory, the valuation has a long way to fall.

**SMCI — Super Micro Computer**
- **Why:** Worth watching as a cautionary tale, not a buy. The stock dropped over 20% after its co-founder was charged with selling servers containing sanctioned NVIDIA hardware to China — a stark reminder that AI infrastructure plays carry geopolitical risk as well as market risk.
- **Bear Case:** The legal exposure here is real and ongoing. If further charges follow, or if regulators tighten export controls more broadly, Super Micro is in a difficult position. This is not a recovery story yet.

**CRWV — CoreWeave**
- **Why:** CoreWeave has emerged as a high-growth AI cloud infrastructure provider with notable insider ownership — often a sign that those closest to the company believe in its trajectory. It's worth keeping an eye on as the AI infrastructure buildout continues.
- **Bear Case:** CoreWeave's entire thesis depends on sustained, aggressive AI investment from hyperscalers. If any of the major cloud players pull back on capex — as some analysts expect could happen in 2026 — CoreWeave's order book could thin out quickly. High insider ownership also means high insider concentration of risk.

**KBH — KB Home**
- **Why:** US homebuilders reported quarterly earnings this week amid a housing market that remains structurally undersupplied. If interest rates continue their gradual decline globally, pent-up demand for housing could benefit builders.
- **Bear Case:** Tariffs on Canadian lumber and imported construction materials are quietly inflating build costs. A homebuilder that can't pass costs on to already-stretched buyers is a homebuilder with shrinking margins.

**SFD — Smithfield Foods**
- **Why:** As a major packaged meat producer, Smithfield is a defensive play — people eat regardless of whether markets are up or down — and it benefits from falling energy input costs if Iran ceasefire talks succeed.
- **Bear Case:** Tariff retaliation from trading partners (particularly China, which imports significant US pork products) could squeeze Smithfield's export revenues hard. Trade wars have a way of turning protein into collateral damage.

## 3. The Expat Angle

For anyone living in Zurich on a British salary or pension, the GBP/CHF picture has been quietly uncomfortable. The pound has traded in a narrow range of roughly 1.03–1.05 against the franc in March 2026, with the year's average sitting around 1.056. That's meaningfully weaker than the 1.10+ levels seen in the early 2020s, which means your pounds buy fewer francs than they used to. The Swiss National Bank held its policy rate at 0% on 19 March — and with Swiss inflation now running at just 0.1% year-on-year as of February, there is very little pressure to raise rates. In fact, the SNB has signalled greater willingness to intervene directly in currency markets to *prevent* the franc from strengthening further, as Middle East tensions drive safe-haven demand for CHF. That's good news if you're already paid in francs; less so if you're converting from GBP. For renters in Zurich, the headline numbers remain brutal — a one-bedroom apartment still averages CHF 1,300–1,650 per month — and with the rental market chronically undersupplied, don't expect any relief from low inflation to translate into lower rents any time soon.

## 4. Critical Risks

- **Tariff escalation spiral:** Section 122 tariffs have a 150-day clock. If they aren't resolved or replaced before expiry, markets will need to price in their continuation — or a new legal battle. The Section 301 investigations now covering dozens of countries could produce a wave of retaliatory tariffs before summer.
- **Oil price whipsaw:** Markets rallied on US-Iran ceasefire optimism, but the Middle East remains acutely unstable. A breakdown in talks — or a new incident — could send oil sharply higher, reigniting the inflation that central banks spent two years trying to tame.
- **CHF safe-haven squeeze:** If geopolitical stress increases, the franc will strengthen further, compounding the GBP/CHF squeeze and forcing the SNB into even more aggressive FX intervention — which it can do, but only at the cost of expanding its already enormous balance sheet.
- **AI capex correction:** Markets are pricing in sustained hyper-investment in AI infrastructure. If any major hyperscaler signals a slowdown in data centre spending, the ripple effect through ARM, CoreWeave, SMCI, and their suppliers could be swift and severe.
- **UK economic fragility:** The pound's relative weakness isn't accidental. UK growth remains sluggish, and the Bank of England faces the same stagflationary bind as most central banks — cut rates and you risk inflation, hold rates and you risk recession. Neither outcome is friendly to GBP.
