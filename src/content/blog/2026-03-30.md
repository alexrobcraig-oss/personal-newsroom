## 1. Macro Summary

The dominant story of March 2026 is one that would be familiar to any student of history: a war in the Middle East is reshaping the global economy in real time. Following US–Israeli strikes on Iran at the end of February, Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz — a narrow waterway through which roughly 20% of the world's oil supply passes. The consequences have been swift and severe. Brent crude hit $107 per barrel on March 26th, up from around $75 just a month ago, a rise of over 40%. LNG gas prices have more than doubled in Asian markets. For consumers across Europe — already nursing the memory of post-pandemic inflation — this is a deeply unwelcome development.

The knock-on effects are spreading fast. The OECD now expects G20 inflation to come in at 4.0% for 2026, a full 1.2 percentage points higher than projected before the conflict. The ECB, which had been cautiously preparing markets for interest rate cuts, reversed course entirely on March 19th and raised its inflation forecast instead. J.P. Morgan now puts the probability of a global recession this year at 35%, and Moody's AI model — which historically has never crossed 50% without a recession following — is currently sitting at 49%. The margin for error is thin. As one analyst put it with unusual candour: the global economy is one bad data print away from the historical tripwire. One suspects Mr. Hitchens, were he watching, would note that the Strait of Hormuz has long been one of those geographical bottlenecks through which the follies of great powers tend to be invoiced.

UK-specific data adds further texture. The Office for Budget Responsibility's March 2026 report projects British GDP growth at 1.4% — sluggish at best — while unemployment has risen to 5.2%, its highest since 2020. UK inflation sits at 3.0%, still above the Bank of England's 2% target, and the OBR's own forecasts were prepared *before* the Iran escalation in late February. In short, the UK's economic headroom is narrower than the official figures suggest.

## 2. Stock Watch

**TSM — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company**
- **Why:** TSMC reported full-year 2025 revenues of $122 billion with record gross margins of 62.3%; high-performance computing (driven by AI chips) now makes up 58% of revenues, making it the closest thing to an infrastructure company for the AI era.
- **Bear Case:** TSMC sits squarely in the Taiwan Strait — if geopolitical tensions between the US and China escalate, the company's physical location becomes its greatest liability, not an abstraction.

**OXY — Occidental Petroleum**
- **Why:** With Brent above $100, US oil producers are printing money; Occidental has strong domestic production and Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway has been a notable long-term buyer.
- **Bear Case:** If diplomatic channels reopen the Strait of Hormuz, oil prices could fall sharply and rapidly — an energy-price rally built on war is not the same as one built on structural demand.

**NEE — NextEra Energy**
- **Why:** Utilities have been the standout sector of March, up over 10%; NextEra is the world's largest producer of wind and solar energy and benefits from both the energy crisis and long-term decarbonisation trends.
- **Bear Case:** Utilities carry heavy debt loads and are rate-sensitive; if central banks are forced to hike rather than cut, NextEra's financing costs rise materially.

**CRWD — CrowdStrike**
- **Why:** Geopolitical conflict invariably raises the threat of state-sponsored cyberattacks; CrowdStrike is the market leader in endpoint security and tends to see enterprise demand spike during global instability.
- **Bear Case:** CrowdStrike's premium valuation leaves little room for disappointment; any high-profile service outage (as occurred in 2024) can cause outsized stock-price damage.

**EXP — Eagle Materials**
- **Why:** US infrastructure spending remains a policy priority across administrations; Eagle Materials produces cement, wallboard, and aggregates — the unglamorous inputs that actually build things.
- **Bear Case:** Rising energy costs directly compress Eagle's margins, since cement production is extraordinarily energy-intensive; the very oil shock creating opportunity in OXY is a headwind here.

## 3. The Expat Angle

For British expats in Zurich, this week's picture is one of quiet Swiss resilience bumping up against a very loud global storm. The SNB held its policy rate at 0% on March 19th — where it has sat since June 2025 — while Swiss inflation barely registers at 0.1%. Switzerland, as ever, is the eye of the hurricane rather than the gale. The franc is appreciating as a safe-haven currency (the SNB has already signalled it may intervene to prevent excessive CHF strength), which is *good* for your purchasing power in CHF terms but *bad* if you're paid in GBP and converting. GBP/CHF has been trading in the 1.01–1.04 range this month, with sterling under pressure from UK economic weakness and rising energy import costs. The gap between Britain's structurally sluggish economy and Switzerland's ultra-low-inflation fortress is, if anything, widening.

## 4. Critical Risks

- **Strait of Hormuz remains closed:** Every additional week of closure tightens the supply crunch; models suggest $150/barrel oil by summer if the blockade persists through the LNG refill season.
- **Inflation re-acceleration in Europe:** The ECB has already paused cuts; if energy prices stay elevated, a rate *hike* cannot be ruled out — which would put pressure on European mortgage holders and bond markets.
- **CHF safe-haven overshoot:** The SNB has limited tools left at zero rates; if the franc appreciates too sharply, Swiss export competitiveness suffers and the SNB may be forced into negative rates — again.
- **Recession probability approaching threshold:** Moody's model at 49% and J.P. Morgan at 35% recession probability — these are not the same as a prediction, but they are not comfortable numbers to ignore.
- **UK stagflation trap:** Rising energy costs, elevated unemployment, and below-target growth create a classic stagflation scenario where the Bank of England can neither cut (inflation too high) nor hike (growth too weak) — a policy cul-de-sac with no clean exit.
