## 1. Macro Summary

Two pressure points are converging this week in a way that should make anyone with savings sit up. First, used vehicle wholesale prices in the US have jumped — and Wolf Richter's warning is pointed: this is exactly how the 2020–2021 inflation episode began, with pipeline pressures that looked transitory right up until they weren't. Tariff disruptions to new car supply chains are the likely culprit this time, pushing buyers into the used market and bidding up prices. If that dynamic runs its course, it feeds into CPI with a lag of roughly two to three months. The Fed, still scarred from being late last time, will be watching closely. So should you.

Meanwhile, Brent crude has pushed to nearly $98 a barrel on fragile ceasefire nerves between the US and Iran — a deal that markets appear to trust roughly as far as they could throw it. Oil at these levels is a tax on everything: transport, manufacturing, heating, food distribution. For a British expat in Switzerland, you're partly insulated by the franc's safe-haven strength, but European energy costs and UK headline inflation (relevant if you hold Sterling assets or have family costs back home) are both sensitive to a sustained move above $100. The two stories together — sticky goods prices and an oil spike — are precisely the combination that turns "inflation is cooling" into "actually, never mind."

On the housing front, Wolf Street's European survey is worth a scan if only to calibrate perspective. Switzerland doesn't feature — it operates under its own gravity — but the broader picture across Germany, France, the Netherlands and Sweden is one of significant real-terms declines from bubble peaks, while markets like Spain and Portugal have held up stubbornly. The lesson, if there is one: European housing is not a monolith, and anyone extrapolating a single trend across the continent is selling something.

## 2. Stock Watch

**[AA] — Alcoa Corporation**
- **Why:** A 22% gain in Q1 reflects genuine commodity demand tailwinds; if oil stays elevated and the energy transition continues requiring aluminium at scale, the fundamental case holds.
- **Bear Case:** Alcoa is leveraged to energy costs (smelting is electricity-intensive), so an oil and power price spike cuts both ways — demand may hold while margins compress.

**[NKE] — Nike**
- **Why:** A 14% Q1 fall has dragged the valuation down to a level where patient investors with a 2–3 year horizon can begin to see a re-rating case if the new management turnaround gains traction.
- **Bear Case:** Nike's China revenue remains under structural pressure from domestic competitors like Anta and Li-Ning, and a prolonged tariff environment disrupts its Asian supply chain in ways that are genuinely hard to model.

**[XOM] — ExxonMobil**
- **Why:** With Brent near $98, integrated majors with upstream exposure are printing cash; the dividend is well-covered and provides a floor even if the thesis takes time.
- **Bear Case:** A genuine, durable Iran ceasefire deal — unlikely but not impossible — could send oil back to the low $80s within weeks and deflate the whole thesis.
